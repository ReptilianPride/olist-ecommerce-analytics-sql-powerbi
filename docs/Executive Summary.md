# Executive Findings & Recommendations

---

## Executive Summary

The business grew strongly through 2017, reaching peak performance in November of that year. The 2018 data tells a different story: active customers and revenue collapsed sharply by late 2018. This was not a seasonal dip. It points to a structural problem. Alongside the 2018 decline, there is a persistent retention issue running across all years: the business brings in new customers but almost none of them return to buy again.

---

## Key Executive Findings

### 1. Growth Was Real but Fragile (2017)
Revenue and customer counts grew consistently throughout 2017, peaking in November. Average order values stayed stable between $155 and $170, which means the growth came from more customers placing orders, not from customers spending more per order. The business was acquiring new customers effectively but had not built a loyal returning base.

### 2. 2018 Started Well, Then Declined Sharply
Early 2018 maintained the strong revenue and customer numbers from the 2017 peak. From August 2018 onward, both fell sharply. Active customers dropped by approximately 99% in September 2018 and revenue followed. The average order value spike in September 2018 is unusual and needs investigation. It may reflect a small number of very large orders during an otherwise near-empty trading period, or it could indicate a data or fulfilment issue.

### 3. Customer Retention is the Core Business Problem
This is the most important finding in the data:

- **Over 95% of customers purchase only once.**
- **The customer repeat rate is just 3%.**
- Cohort retention falls below 1% after the first purchase period.
- Most repeat buyers order twice in total. Customers who order three or more times are extremely rare.
- The majority of customers generate under $200 in total lifetime revenue. Very few exceed $500.

The business currently operates as a one-transaction funnel. Customers come in once and do not return. This is not a sustainable model.

### 4. Geographic and Category Concentration Risk
Sao Paulo accounts for the highest number of deliveries and cancellations, followed by Rio de Janeiro. The overall cancellation rate of 0.8% is low. However, heavy reliance on two cities means that any disruption in either location has a large impact on overall performance. The top five product categories (bed, bath and home; health and beauty; sports and leisure; tech accessories; home furniture and decor) account for the large majority of all orders. The bottom five categories contribute almost nothing to total volume.

### 5. 2016 Data is Insufficient for Analysis
The 2016 dataset covers only the final months of the year. It does not provide enough information for reliable trend analysis. All findings and recommendations are based on 2017 and 2018 data.

---

## Recommendations

### Priority 1: Investigate the 2018 Collapse (Immediate)
A 99% drop in active customers in a single month is not a normal pattern. Before any growth planning can take place, the root cause must be identified:

- Was there a failure in the platform, payment system, or delivery network?
- Was there a major reduction in marketing spend or a change in acquisition channels?
- Does the data reflect a real loss of customers or a problem with how data was recorded?

**No growth strategy should be set until this question is answered.**

---

### Priority 2: Build a Post-Purchase Retention Programme (0 to 3 Months)
With 95% of customers buying only once, even a small improvement in repeat purchase rates will have a significant effect on revenue.

- Set up an automated follow-up email sequence after each purchase: order confirmation, delivery update, and a replenishment prompt at 30, 60, and 90 days.
- Introduce a loyalty or rewards scheme aimed at customers after their first purchase.
- Test a discount or bundle offer for a second purchase. The data shows this is where the largest drop-off in customer activity occurs.

---

### Priority 3: Identify and Protect High-Value Customers (0 to 3 Months)
A small number of customers spend more than $500 over their lifetime. These customers generate significantly more revenue than the average and are likely not receiving dedicated attention.

- Identify high lifetime value customers in the CRM and flag them for priority service.
- Offer personalised outreach, early access to new products, or dedicated support.
- Analyse what these customers have in common (product category, location, acquisition channel) to improve targeting for future customer acquisition.

---

### Priority 4: Focus Resources on Top-Performing Categories (3 to 6 Months)
The top five categories drive the overwhelming majority of order volume. Concentrating investment here will deliver the most reliable return.

- Maintain strong inventory depth and availability in these categories.
- Direct promotional and marketing spend toward them.
- Strengthen supplier relationships to protect margins and ensure reliable fulfilment.

The bottom five categories should be reviewed. Unless they serve a specific purpose such as supporting cross-sell activity or delivering strong margins, they are candidates for discontinuation.

---

### Priority 5: Reduce Geographic Concentration (3 to 6 Months)
Sao Paulo and Rio de Janeiro together account for the majority of all activity. This creates risk as well as opportunity.

- Launch targeted campaigns in other Brazilian cities to spread the revenue base.
- Ensure logistics and customer support capacity in Sao Paulo is sufficient to handle its volume reliably.

---

### Priority 6: Grow Average Order Value (3 to 6 Months)
Average order value has been stable at $155 to $170 but has not grown. Since volume-led growth alone proved unsustainable, increasing how much each customer spends per order is an important additional lever.

- Introduce product bundles and cross-sell suggestions at the checkout stage, focused on top-performing categories.
- Test a free shipping threshold set above the current average order value (for example, free shipping above $185 or $200) to encourage customers to add more to their basket.
